About S.T.R.I

STRI is a gazetteer of the words and phrases used to render terms for Roman institutions and Roman legal technical terminology in extant Syriac texts, with special focus on texts composed prior to the Arab conquests in the seventh century, though it will incorporate evidence from texts written in the Islamic period as well.

With the support of a Seed Grant from the Center for Digital Humanities at Princeton University, the prototype will serve as a proof of concept for the full version of STRI. The prototype will have a corpus limited to a single text, the Chronicle of Pseudo-Joshua the Stylite (henceforth, CPJ), and a gazetteer of the terms that occur in CPJ.

What's unique about S.T.R.I?





Syriac Terms For Roman Institutions

S.T.R.I grew out of an interest in investigating the ways in which Syriac-speakers and Syriac-speaking communities participated in the Roman empire and the Roman world. Unlike other online lexica, databases, and digital resources for the Syriac language, STRI is built for both specialists who want to dive deep into the lexical complexities of Syriac in its Greco-Roman contexts and non-specialists who may have no knowledge of Syriac, Greek, Latin, or any other ancient language, but who are interested in learning more about Syriac.




S.T.R.I, however, is not a dictionary!

It is a tool for scholars who want to engage with Syriac texts and use lexical data from Syriac literature. Right now, the prototype has a corpus of only one text, the Chronicle of Pseudo-Joshua the Stylite, which was written sometime in the first two decades of the sixth century CE during the reign of the Roman emperor Anastasius (the anonymous author’s hero).




The long-term goals of STRI

They include incorporating data from the entire Syriac corpus (that’s a lot!), Syriac inscriptions, and unpublished Syriac manuscripts, as well as lexical data from the Thesaurus Linguae Graecae, Simtho, and several other online repositories. We are also exploring ways to develop fully annotated, tagged, and lemmatized digital editions of the major works of Syriac literature. Of course, this is a huge goal and one that will require much collaboration. As a result, collaboration and extensibility will be key to ensuring that STRI will continue to grow in the future.
